Grocery Guide — Where to Buy What

Your store loop, seeded from the research: Costco monthly for protein volume → Sprouts weekly for produce → farmers market when the schedule allows. Prices below are from basket-test articles (blog-quality — treat as directional, not gospel).

🏬 Costco — the protein base (monthly run)

  • Buy: chicken breast, ground beef, eggs (bulk), Greek yogurt, Chobani 20g protein drinks, frozen fruit, rice, oats, canned fish.
  • Best price-per-gram of protein of your four stores; freeze what you won't cook this week.

🥬 Sprouts — the produce run (weekly)

  • Buy: fresh vegetables, fruit, bulk bins (nuts, oats, grains), sale-cycle meat specials.
  • Basket tests put Sprouts ~$245–285/wk vs Whole Foods ~$295–355 for a comparable family basket — produce and bulk are where it wins.

🛒 Whole Foods — selective stops

  • Buy: eggs/dairy staples when convenient (it won some staple-basket tests), specialty items, 365-brand basics. Prime discounts narrow the gap.
  • Skip for: bulk protein and general produce runs — you're paying a premium.

🌽 Farmers market — quality & season

  • Buy: in-season produce, eggs when available. It's a quality/freshness play, not a protein value play.
  • Bonus: walking the market on Sat/Sun doubles as your active-recovery day from the Weekly Plan.

🌯 Eating out late (after-work fallback)

  • Chipotle double-chicken bowl, no rice/beans: ~50g P / ~450 cal.
  • Chick-fil-A Grilled Cool Wrap (no dressing): ~42g P / ~350 cal.
  • Rule of thumb: named grilled protein + vegetables, skip liquid calories.

Guidance — What Actually Works

Techniques below are drawn from behavioral research on habit change and addiction recovery. They apply to alcohol, porn, nicotine, junk food — most compulsive habits share the same loop: trigger → urge → ritual → relief → regret. You break the loop, not the person.

1. Urge surfing

Urges peak and pass like a wave — usually within 15–20 minutes. Instead of fighting or obeying the urge, observe it: where do you feel it, how strong is it 1–10, is it rising or falling? Naming it ("this is an urge, not a command") reliably weakens it.

2. Map your triggers

  • Time: late night is the most common window for both drinking and porn. Change what bedtime looks like — phone outside the bedroom is the single highest-leverage move for porn specifically.
  • Place: if it always happens in one spot (bed, couch, car), change the environment or your routine there.
  • State: HALT — Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired. Most relapses happen in one of these states. Fix the state, not the urge.

3. Raise the friction

Willpower is a bad gatekeeper; environment is a good one. Remove alcohol from the house. Use app blockers / DNS filtering for adult content and put the passcode in someone else's hands or a delay lock. Every extra step between urge and habit cuts follow-through dramatically.

4. Replace, don't just remove

The habit was serving something — stress relief, boredom, loneliness, dopamine. Slot a replacement into the exact same trigger window: a lift, a walk, a cold shower, calling someone, even a chore. Your gym habit is your best weapon here — schedule workouts in your danger windows.

5. Track and forecast

That's what this page is for. Streak data reveals your patterns: if resets cluster on Friday nights, that's not weakness, that's a schedule problem you can engineer around. After a reset, log what happened right before — it's your most valuable data point.

6. Don't rely on supplements

No vitamin or supplement is proven to stop compulsive habits. The basics that genuinely help regulation: consistent sleep, resistance training, sunlight in the morning, protein-adequate diet, and limiting late-night screen time. If a deficiency is suspected (vitamin D is common), confirm with a blood test rather than guessing.

7. Know when to get help

If quitting alcohol causes shakes, sweats, or anxiety spikes, talk to a doctor — withdrawal can be medically serious and shouldn't be done cold-turkey alone. For any habit that keeps beating your best efforts, therapy (especially CBT/ACT) and groups like SMART Recovery are force multipliers, not last resorts.

💡 The 1% rule you mentioned: a streak isn't the goal — the identity is. "I'm someone who doesn't drink on weeknights" beats "I'm on day 12." Days are the scoreboard, not the game.
This page is general educational content, not medical or mental-health advice. For withdrawal symptoms or a habit affecting your safety, work, or relationships, talk to a healthcare professional.
